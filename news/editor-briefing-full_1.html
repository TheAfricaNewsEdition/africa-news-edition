Chad / Military
France Quietly Prepares Return to Chad After 18-Month Expulsion
After Chadian President Mahamat Idriss Déby expelled around 1,000 French soldiers in January 2025, citing national sovereignty, Paris and N'Djamena have now been working toward renewed military partnership. Their goal is to rebuild a more discreet, mutually beneficial relationship without the permanent French presence that previously characterized their cooperation.
Déby found the alternatives on offer—support from Emiratis, Turks, and Russians—unsatisfactory. The Emiratis became problematic due to their support for paramilitary groups in Sudan's civil war; Turkish drones proved too expensive for limited operational value; and poor results of Russian forces in Mali discouraged broader partnership. France, by comparison, is viewed as a historic and reliable ally with a demonstrated commitment to regional stability.

Yale Investigation Exposes Ethiopian Base Weaponizing Vehicles for Sudan's RSF
A new investigation by Yale School of Public Health's Humanitarian Research Lab has exposed an active military logistics chain inside an Ethiopian National Defense Forces base in Asosa, where civilian vehicles are systematically modified, armed, and painted to supply Sudan's Rapid Support Forces.
The report, based on satellite imagery analyzed between March 29 and May 21, 2026, reveals a large-scale refitting operation. Analysts repeatedly identified clusters of narrow, dark objects approximately 1.6 meters long, situated next to vehicles with mounting equipment. Based on exact dimensions and positioning, the report assesses with high confidence that these objects are heavy machine guns (50-calibre) being affixed to modified trucks.

Kenya / Legal Innovation
First War Crimes Complaint Against Sudan's RSF Filed in Kenya
Twelve Sudanese survivors of torture, rape, sexual slavery, and arbitrary detention filed a landmark criminal complaint on June 9 with Kenya's Director of Public Prosecutions, seeking charges against ten identified members of Sudan's Rapid Support Forces for crimes committed in and around Khartoum between April 2023 and March 2025, when the RSF controlled large parts of the capital.
The complaint was filed by Legal Action Worldwide and the African Centre for Justice and Peace Studies and represents the first known attempt to invoke universal jurisdiction in Kenya against alleged perpetrators of atrocity crimes committed abroad. Attorneys noted Sudan's domestic justice system is "inaccessible, unavailable, and ineffective," and that the ICC's mandate in Sudan is limited to Darfur, leaving crimes in Khartoum without an international forum.

Inside Mozambique's State-Sponsored Machinery of Repression
Mozambique's opposition movements demand justice and accountability. Supporters of opposition leader Venâncio Mondlane continue to mobilize despite government crackdowns and repression.
For five months, more than 30 journalists from 10 media organizations collaborated to expose the state-sponsored machinery of repression in Mozambique. The consortium's reporting suggests there is a pattern of political persecution, with dozens of cases pointing to opposition members being targeted by actors linked to FRELIMO and Mozambique's security forces, particularly in the aftermath of the October 2024 general elections.

South African civil society and affected communities mobilize against xenophobia. Demonstrators call for recognition of African solidarity and human rights protection.
Princess Adjei, 33, set up her hair salon in downtown Durban in November after living in South Africa since moving from Ghana as a toddler—there was no other place she called home. Abdullah Ibrahim, who helped define a genre of South African jazz music, has died at the age of 91. He changed his name after converting to Islam in the late 1960s.

Geopolitics / Trade
China Embeds Itself in Africa's Maritime Network
China accounts for roughly 22% of all African trade, and Chinese firms now operate, finance, partner with, or hold stakes in roughly one-third of African ports. Through its merchant fleet—the largest in the world—as well as state-owned shipping and construction firms, terminal operators, technology companies, and policy banks, China has become increasingly embedded in the systems that underpin African maritime activity.
China's expanding military activity in dual-use ports that serve both commercial and naval purposes could entangle African countries in geostrategic rivalries and undermine African efforts to diversify their security partnerships. Africa faces a central tension from China's deepening engagement in Africa's port and trade corridors: expanded capacity may result in economic benefits, but it also comes with the risk of reduced autonomy over critical infrastructure as well as altered governance and regulatory norms.

Disruption caused by the Iran war is weakening smartphone availability in Africa and making handsets more expensive. Rising costs of components, supply chain constraints, and weaker consumer demand partly linked to the Iran war have depressed smartphone sales in much of the continent, resulting in the slowest growth of shipments to Africa in two years.
