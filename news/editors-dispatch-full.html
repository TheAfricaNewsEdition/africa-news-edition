Editor's Weekly Dispatch

Scattered Thoughts & Reflections from Another Week Covering Africa

By Nasser Ega-Musa, Editor, The Africa News Edition

22–28 June 2026

News and Reflections

Relocating Le Tricolore

The news that Burkina Faso has completely broken off diplomatic ties with France shouldn't alarm fans of 37 Quai d'Orsay, who are already burning candles at both ends trying to devise a suitable punishment. I suspect most of the French diplomatic staff in Ouagadougou will simply be relocated to Nairobi. Kenya is rapidly becoming Macron's preferred base for raising le Tricolore in East Africa—safely removed from those pesky, ungrateful West African Francophones.

Tshisekedi's Courtroom Deflection

With the Democratic Republic of Congo taking Rwanda to the International Court of Justice over the conflict in its eastern provinces, President Félix Tshisekedi continues what appears to be a familiar tradition among Congolese leaders since independence in 1960: deflecting from domestic failures by externalizing blame. The DRC continues to insist that every major problem begins in Kigali. To many regional observers, however, Tshisekedi's legal initiative appears to be a transparent attempt to build political cover for constitutional changes that would allow him to extend his presidency. Few at home are falling for it, and fewer abroad (save for France and Belgium) are buying it. The case should, and will likely, be thrown out with prejudice.

Photo: Uganda's de facto military leader, General Muhoozi Kainerugaba, at a public address. His control of state institutions raises questions about the boundaries between military power and civilian governance in the region.

Uganda's Open Secret

Uganda's president in all but name, General Muhoozi Kainerugaba—the military chief who also happens to be the ageing and fading incumbent's son—announced that he personally ordered the closure of two leading media outlets, declaring that he does "not believe in a free press." Only a de facto ruler could routinely use social media to boast about detaining opponents, threaten to "remove the testicles" of a leading presidential candidate, Bobi Wine, and spark diplomatic incidents by threatening to invade Kenya, all the while everyone pretends he isn't the president. Why the theatre?

The Great Minnesota Scapegoat

The supposed "deputy" (a Somali man) to the master mind (a White woman) of Covid funds fraud in Minnesota has been arrested in Somalia by the FBI. The media takeaway? Any massive fraud committed in Minnesota during the Biden era is being conveniently subscribed to Somalis. Really? This is the news being touted by an administration whose head and his cohorts have 'misdirecting' tens of billions through opaque channels since 2025.

The Unsurprising BBC Story of the Week

Tanzania has once again suspended political rallies just three years after lifting the ban. How could anyone be surprised after:

  • The severe crackdown on opposition voices;
  • Suppression of public dissent;
  • Security forces using lethal force against demonstrators;
  • Widespread enforced disappearances;
  • Blocking international human rights investigators; and
  • Imprisoning opposition leader Tundu Lissu on treason charges?

Tanzania's authoritarianism no longer appears to be on a slow boil—it seems to be running at full throttle.

Kenya's Ebola Facility About-Face

The controversial U.S.-backed Ebola hospital project in Kenya has been abruptly abandoned. What tipped the balance? Was it the High Court intervention, sustained public opposition, or the Health Minister being held in contempt for ignoring court orders? The climax came when the minister personally appeared before the judge, apologized, and announced an immediate halt to construction. Something had clearly gone badly wrong. A rare—if messy—victory for Kenyan civil society and judicial independence? Or merely a pause until public attention shifts elsewhere?

South Africa's Privatized Sovereignty

As South Africa prepared for highly charged anti-illegal immigration marches ahead of a self-declared 30 June deadline issued by vigilante groups, the government's response was revealing. Pretoria redirected R600 million from the police budget to manage expected unrest, while major private security companies openly prepared to serve as "force multipliers."

When a country maintains roughly three private security guards for every police officer, public safety ceases to be a constitutional guarantee—it becomes a commercial service.

Photo: South Africans gather in a demonstration against xenophobic violence. As civil unrest intensifies, African governments have begun airlifting nationals from South Africa, marking a sobering moment for continental solidarity.

The Arab Spring's Final Embers

Tunisia's judiciary has sentenced prominent human rights activist Sihem Bensedrine to 25 years in prison. Once head of the Truth and Dignity Commission investigating historic state abuses, she has now become another casualty of President Kais Saied's systematic dismantling of political dissent. The message from Tunis seems unmistakable: look too closely into the mirror of authoritarianism, and you may spend decades behind bars.

Nairobi on Lockdown

Kenyan police demonstrated one of their more familiar competencies: shutting down central Nairobi. Horse patrols, plain-clothes officers, and extensive roadblocks prevented a memorial march marking the second anniversary of the Gen Z protests. The Interior Minister proudly announced 355 nationwide arrests, claiming authorities had stopped "goons and criminals." Curious how a government that champions democratic freedoms abroad often treats domestic grief as a national security threat.

Accountability on the Auction Block

Sudan's strategic city of El Obeid continues suffering relentless RSF drone attacks that have crippled electricity and hospital services. Simultaneously, several senior RSF commanders reportedly defected to the Sudanese Armed Forces. While government supporters celebrated these apparent public relations victories, survivors of atrocities in Darfur asked a more difficult question:

Is justice quietly being sacrificed for military expediency?

The silence may itself be the answer.

Photo: Health workers in protective gear conduct response operations during the DRC's Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak. Despite international pledges of $910 million, only 13% has reached frontline responders as of June 2026.

Ebola: The Forgotten Outbreak

While global attention remains elsewhere, the Democratic Republic of Congo's Bundibugyo Ebola outbreak has quietly reached:

1,139
Confirmed Cases
293
Confirmed Deaths

Because this involves the rare Bundibugyo strain, there is still no approved vaccine or standardized treatment. Although approximately $910 million has reportedly been pledged internationally, only around 13% has reached frontline responders. Combined with active conflict and displacement, contact tracing remains dangerously inadequate. Public health sovereignty remains an expensive luxury when permanent humanitarian crises become normalized.

Africans Being Cruel to Fellow Africans

Among this week's more depressing themes is violence directed at African migrants by fellow Africans. South Africa continues experiencing organized attacks against migrants. Meanwhile, Libya has expanded efforts to detain, expel, and abandon African migrants in the Sahara, amid longstanding allegations of torture, rape, forced labor, and even modern slave markets. History, it seems, occasionally refuses to remain history.

The High Price of a Tweet

A Somali court sentenced a woman to three years in prison for criticizing the government online. In a country confronting genuine security threats, it is striking where state resources are allocated. Apparently, fragile political egos demand greater protection than ordinary citizens. Elon Musk can probably be grateful he isn't Somali.

Can Xenophobia be Extinguished by Royalty?

South Africa has endured weeks of xenophobic violence that has already claimed lives and forced several African governments to repatriate thousands of their nationals. Pause and consider that. African countries are now airlifting their own citizens out of another African country because they fear for their safety. President Cyril Ramaphosa has appealed to traditional monarchs to use their moral authority to calm tensions. One hopes the kings and queens prove more persuasive than the government itself.

842 Killed, 279 Kidnapped

According to Nextier's Nigeria Violent Conflicts Database, May 2026 alone saw:

  • 842 killed
  • 279 kidnapped
  • 156 violent incidents

Deaths reportedly increased 90% compared with the same month last year. Nearly 35 million Nigerians now face severe food insecurity. At some point, the government in Abuja must stop governing by press release.

Mending Fences in the Sahel

Following high-level talks in Cotonou, Niger and Benin are attempting to normalize relations after nearly three years of border closures following Niger's 2023 coup. Economic necessity appears finally to be outweighing political symbolism, particularly as jihadist violence intensifies on both sides of the frontier.

Zimbabwe's Forever Presidency

Zimbabwe's Senate has approved constitutional amendments effectively extending presidential tenure. Critics immediately labeled the move a constitutional coup. It's the classic liberation-party playbook: rewrite the supreme law of the land under the guise of stability, ensuring the old guard never has to pack their bags. Democracy in Zimbabwe remains an elaborate game of musical chairs where the music never stops for the ruling elite. The government calls it "enhancing political stability." Everyone else calls it what it is.

Morocco's "Escobar of the Sahara"

Two former senior Moroccan politicians have received lengthy prison sentences for involvement in a trans-Saharan drug trafficking network. For years they reportedly presented themselves as guardians of public service while facilitating organized crime. It's a stark reminder that the distinction between state institutions and criminal enterprises can, sometimes, become remarkably thin.

The Reparations Strategy

At Christiansborg Castle in Accra, African and Caribbean leaders adopted a unified strategy calling for formal apologies and reparations for the transatlantic slave trade. The UN resolution that preceded this conference received:

  • 123 votes in favor
  • 3 against (United States, Israel, Argentina)
  • 52 abstentions, including every EU member.

Fifty-two European abstentions.

France, despite its frequent emphasis on human rights diplomacy, also abstained. But the world moved on anyway.

What To Believe In Sudan

Both the RSF and Sudan Armed Forces are reportedly using artificial intelligence, influencer campaigns, and coordinated disinformation to shape perceptions of the conflict. War in the 21st century now comes with a media strategy, and increasingly includes psychological operations conducted through social media. Sudanese civilians now endure not only the world's largest humanitarian crisis but also an information war designed to confuse and manipulate them.

China's Zero-Tariff Bait

Analysts continue debating whether China's expanded zero-tariff policy will genuinely benefit African small businesses or simply deepen structural dependence. Meanwhile, the IMF warns of another tightening debt cycle. Africa again finds itself choosing between:

  • Western structural adjustment programs, and
  • Eastern resource-backed infrastructure financing.

One cannot help wondering whether a genuine third option might someday emerge.

A Salute to Royal Cattle in Kigali

While legal battles with the DRC intensify, Rwandan state media devoted attention to the country's royal Inyambo cattle, celebrated with traditional poetry and classical music. There is something wonderfully surreal about a government executing hyper-modern regional geopolitics while simultaneously ensuring its cows receive a finer cultural education than the average internet troll.

Libya's Unbothered Feline

In a rare piece of non-political news out of Libya, a random YouTube video accidentally proved that the elusive sand cat—long feared extinct in the region—is alive and well. In a country that has been fractured by rival militias, oil blockades, and foreign intervention for over a decade, it turns out a tiny, unbothered desert cat is the only entity successfully navigating the chaos.

Dangote's Net Worth Flex

Aliko Dangote argued publicly that Forbes and Bloomberg significantly underestimate his wealth because his most valuable industrial assets remain privately held. At a moment when millions across Africa struggle with food inflation, currency instability, and persistent hunger, it's deeply comforting to know that our billionaires are fighting the good fight: correcting the record on just how obscenely rich they actually are. The disconnect between Africa's billionaire class and its masses scarcely requires additional commentary.

Ojude Oba: A Blueprint for Unity

In an ancient town in southwest Nigeria, thousands gathered for the spectacular Ojude Oba festival in Ijebu-Ode to pay homage to the Awujale—the traditional ruler of the Ijebu people. This incredible display of culture and tradition, meaning "the king's forecourt," began over two centuries ago. Originally a Muslim gathering earmarked for the third day after Eid, it has grown to symbolize pride, unity, and identity among all Ijebu people, regardless of religion. Africa: can we please have many more traditions that unite rather than divide.

The Polygamist: An Unexpected Hit

Was I the only person unaware of Netflix's The Polygamist? The 22-episode Zulu-language series, about the tangled love life of wealthy Johannesburg businessman Jonasi Gomora, is all the rage from Lagos to Nairobi to Hollywood, where even some 'A'-listers are hooked and binging on it. The show also made it to the top 10 in Mauritius within the first week of its release. It attracted two million views and was number four on Netflix's top 10 list for non-English series globally, also in the first week. Beyond Africa, it was among the most watched shows in Trinidad and Tobago, Romania and the Dominican Republic among others. One interesting footnote: among the executive producers are two daughters of former South African president Jacob Zuma—South Africa's former president and a proud polygamist who currently has four wives, been married six times and is estimated to have 20 children.

The 2026 World Cup

Africa's New Standard

Ten African teams qualified for the 2026 World Cup. Nine are advancing to the knockout stage—a phenomenal achievement. For decades, African football celebrated simply qualifying for the tournament. Now we're beginning to feel disappointed if we don't dominate. That's progress.

The Bafana Bafana Conundrum

Among those nine teams is South Africa. During Bafana Bafana's opening match against Mexico, it often seemed as though the rest of the continent was cheering for Mexico. Will Africa soften and rally behind Bafana Bafana now, or are we still collectively too frustrated by South Africa's recurring waves of xenophobia to offer them much support during their match against Canada? And what of those back home preparing to expel fellow Africans? Will they suffer a minor identity crisis when 22-year-old fullback Ime Okon—born to a Nigerian father and a South African mother—takes the field in South African colours?

Photo: African national team players celebrate their World Cup qualification and knockout stage advancement. Nine African teams advancing represents a historic moment—African football has shifted from celebrating qualification to expecting dominance.

Cape Verde's Giant-Killing Spree

Somewhere, there is undoubtedly a FIFA executive explaining—with colourful charts and PowerPoint slides—why only "big markets" truly matter. Meanwhile, Cape Verde, with a population of roughly 600,000, has stunned world football by charging deep into the knockout stages. It turns out football occasionally still rewards organisation, discipline, and belief over GDP. For a diaspora stretching from the United Kingdom to Massachusetts, this is a moment of pure, unadulterated ecstasy. It is also a reminder that when West African talent is not systematically drained or overlooked by European scouting networks, it is more than capable of competing on football's biggest stage. Can we please have a one-off Europe XI vs. West Africa XI? You already know where my money would be.

Central American Focus, European Blindness

I have had friends comment to me—in breathless amazement—that Ecuador is fielding an all-Black football team in the World Cup while its supporters in the stands are virtually all White. They also didn't miss noticing the majority-Black squads of Panama and Colombia. It appears, however, that their eyes were firmly fixed on Central and South America and never risked drifting toward the French and English national teams.

Ninety Minutes of African Unity

The World Cup has once again reminded us that Africans can unite passionately—for ninety minutes. If only constitutional reform, regional integration, and the free movement of people generated even half the excitement of a World Cup match.
