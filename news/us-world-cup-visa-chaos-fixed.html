SPECIAL: WORLD CUP CHAOS

The US' World Cup Travel Warnings Are All Coming True

Players, referees, and officials are being banned, denied entry, searched on the runway, or checked with sniffer dogs upon entering the US for the 2026 World Cup—before a ball has even been kicked.

FIFA President Gianni Infantino presents President Trump with the inaugural FIFA Peace Prize in December 2025—weeks before widespread visa denials began affecting World Cup participants.

Here is a partial list of what has been happening on the eve of the world's greatest football tournament before a ball has even been kicked—a chronicle of administrative chaos, inconsistent enforcement, and troubling precedent-setting that raises serious questions about FIFA's commitment to inclusive global sports governance.

"The international community is watching. These are not security threats. These are world-class athletes and officials being treated like criminals."

The Pattern of Denials

Senegal and Uzbekistan squads were forced to undergo security screening on the tarmac in 31°C (87.8°F) heat rather than inside the cooler airport building—a symbolic but meaningful distinction that raised questions about intentional discomfort and differential treatment.

Omar Artan, officially recognized as Africa's best referee and the 2025 CAF Men's Referee of the Year, was sent back from Miami despite traveling on a diplomatic passport—an extraordinary breach of international protocol that sent shockwaves through global football governance.

Aymen Hussein, an Iraq World Cup star, was held for seven hours by US immigration officials and subjected to intensive questioning that his team characterized as unnecessarily adversarial. The Iraq team photographer was similarly denied entry despite holding a valid visa.

Journalist Concerns
The AIPS (International Association of Sports Press) formally called on FIFA to address what they characterized as "unacceptable visa issues" preventing African and Iranian journalists from covering the tournament. Their warnings went largely unheeded.

FIFA's Non-Response

When confronted with evidence of widespread visa denials and discriminatory treatment, FIFA issued a stark statement: "FIFA is not involved in host country immigration policy, and decisions made by the United States regarding visa eligibility will not be reversed."

This abdication of responsibility stands in sharp contrast to FIFA's aggressive interventionism on other matters. Two years ago, FIFA stripped Indonesia of the U-20 World Cup because the Indonesian government would not grant visas to Israel. At that moment, FIFA suddenly became deeply invested in national visa policies.

When the U.K. considered denying North Koreans visas to play at the 1966 World Cup, FIFA told them they would immediately relocate the tournament. The UK backed down.

The double standard is undeniable. FIFA can move mountains when it suits them. Yet when African referees, Asian journalists, and Middle Eastern athletes face categorical denial, FIFA suddenly becomes powerless and uninvolved.

The Scale of the Crisis

90% of Moroccan fans with valid World Cup tickets have been denied entry to the United States. 14 members of Iran's backroom staff have also had their visas revoked. Switzerland's star player, Breel Embolo, was prevented for three days from even boarding his team's flight from Zurich to Los Angeles until his country formally complained.

According to observers monitoring the situation, US immigration officials apparently confused Ebola with Embolo—suggesting a level of incompetence or carelessness that should alarm anyone concerned with professional standards in government.

Iran National Team Status
The US refused to even host Iran's national team, forcing them to base operations in Tijuana, Mexico instead. Iran must fly in on the day of each match. Additionally, the US revoked Iran's World Cup ticket allocation for supporters—preventing Iranian fans from attending their own nation's games.

This is a World Cup like no other. The tournament has become defined not by football excellence or athletic achievement, but by administrative dysfunction, discriminatory visa enforcement, and the stark collapse of FIFA's supposed commitment to global inclusion.

The Hypocrisy Question

Do you recall all the fuss Western media made about human rights, LGBTQ+ rights, and workers' rights in Qatar? Editorial boards across Europe and North America published thousands of columns condemning Qatar's record and questioning whether FIFA should have awarded them the tournament.

Yet now, on US soil, categorical discrimination against entire nations is unfolding in real time—and the same media outlets have been largely silent. Infantino awarded Trump with the inaugural FIFA Peace Prize in early December 2025. The timing and symbolism were unmistakable.

Do you remember the principle? Or was it ever really about human rights?

The Historical Parallel

1966: The African Boycott

African nations collectively boycotted the 1966 World Cup to protest a discriminatory FIFA ruling that forced the entire African continent, along with Asia and Oceania, to compete for just one solitary qualifying spot. In contrast, Europe was awarded 10 guaranteed places.

Additionally, there were simmering geopolitical tensions as CAF had recently demanded the exclusion of South Africa from FIFA competitions due to its government's apartheid policies.

Led by influential figures like Ghanaian sports director Ohene Djan and Ethiopian CAF administrator Tessema Yidnekatchew, the newly independent African nations used the boycott as a powerful political tool to demand respect and equal representation on the global sporting stage.
