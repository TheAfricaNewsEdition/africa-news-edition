THE AFRICA NEWS EDITION

Perspectives from the Continent and Beyond

To paraphrase Mario Barlotelli: Why Is It Always Us?

There are days when I genuinely wonder whether Somalis have some sort of special arrangement with the international news cycle.

Not a good arrangement, mind you.

Just an arrangement.

Because somehow, wherever there is trouble, confusion, controversy, misunderstanding, political drama, immigration debate, identity crisis, diplomatic row, sporting dispute, or public argument, there is a reasonable chance that a Somali will be found somewhere in the vicinity.

Sometimes we are the story.

Sometimes we are blamed for the story.

And sometimes the story has absolutely nothing to do with us, but we get dragged into it anyway.

Take the latest widely publicised controversy surrounding the 2026 World Cup. Africa's top-rated football referee, a Somali, found himself banned by the US from entering the country to officiate at the tournament because of restrictions imposed by the host nation, that shining global beacon of openness and tolerance known as the United States.

At roughly the same time, Belfast has been grappling with anti-immigrant riots following a knife attack allegedly committed by a Somali.

Except the suspect wasn't Somali.

He was Sudanese.

But to many people, that distinction appears to be a minor administrative detail. To the untrained eye, we all apparently come from the Republic of Looking Alike.

Meanwhile, in Kenya, reports surfaced of a Kenyan Somali being denied entry to a resort because, according to the establishment, some Somalis had caused trouble there in the past.

Not him, of course.

Just Somalis.

Collectively.

As a concept.

Then there is South Africa, where Somali-owned businesses have become fixtures of township economies. In many places, Somali traders have succeeded where larger and better-funded local competitors have struggled. Success, however, can be a dangerous thing. Economic competition has often mixed with resentment, helping fuel recurring waves of xenophobic violence. Somalis frequently find themselves among the victims.

Cross the Atlantic there is Congresswoman Ilhan Omar, perhaps the most famous Somali in America. Whether one agrees with her politics or not, she has become a lightning rod in America's culture wars. Her battles with Donald Trump elevated her from a member of Congress into something larger: a symbol. To supporters she represents immigrant success. To critics she represents everything they dislike about modern America. Either way, her Somali identity is rarely left out of the discussion.

And then there was the Covid-era fraud scandal in Minnesota. A relatively small group of individuals was implicated in wrongdoing. Yet media coverage often left the impression that every Somali in Minneapolis had apparently spent the pandemic plotting the downfall of the American taxpayer.

One could be forgiven for wondering whether CNN, Fox News, the BBC, and sites like TikTok and Facebook, operate a joint "Somali Affairs Desk."

At times it genuinely feels like Somali stories are everywhere.

The question is why.

Part of the answer lies in a historical tragedy.

When the Somali state collapsed in 1991, Somalis scattered across the world on a scale few African communities have experienced. Today, one can find sizeable Somali populations in Britain, Scandinavia, Australia, the Netherlands, Germany, Canada, the United States, Kenya, South Africa, the Gulf states, and countless other places.

We became a global people.

The upside is that Somali communities can now be found almost everywhere. Every airport has Somali taxi drivers.

The downside is that Somali communities can now be found almost everywhere.

If a controversy erupts in Minneapolis, there are Somalis there.

If immigration becomes an issue in London, there are Somalis there.

If riots break out in Belfast, there are Somalis there.

If business competition turns ugly in Johannesburg, there are Somalis there.

If a football referee is denied entry to a World Cup in North America, chances are he is a Somali there too.

We are no longer a story confined to the Horn of Africa.

We have become part of dozens of national stories simultaneously.

But there is another factor.

Somalis are not especially good at being invisible.

Some immigrant communities quietly blend into the background. Somalis, by contrast, tend to arrive with opinions. Strong opinions. Opinions about politics, religion, economics, football, family matters, and occasionally the weather.

We argue loudly.

We organise quickly.

We start businesses.

We enter politics.

We create community organisations.

And when someone says something we dislike, we generally do not suffer in silence.

This tendency can be exhausting.

It can also be admirable.

Either way, it makes us noticeable.

Perhaps that is the real explanation.

Not that Somalis are uniquely troublesome.

Not that the world is uniquely obsessed with Somalis.

But that history scattered us across the globe at precisely the moment when migration, identity, race, religion, and belonging became some of the defining political issues of our age.

We ended up standing at the crossroads of all of them.

The result is that Somalis seem to appear everywhere in the headlines.

And yet there is an irony here.

For every Somali who makes the evening news, there are thousands who never will. They are driving taxis in Toronto, running shops in Cape Town, staffing hospitals in London, attending universities in Stockholm, teaching in Nairobi, serving in public office in Minnesota, and building lives for their families in countries their parents never imagined calling home.

Those stories rarely become headlines.

Conflict sells.

Controversy attracts clicks.

Normality does not.

So perhaps the next time another Somali story explodes across the news cycle, we should resist the temptation to ask, "Why is it always us?"

The better question might be:

How could it not be?

When a people have been scattered to every corner of the earth, sooner or later they become part of every story.

Sometimes for the better.

Sometimes for the worse.

And sometimes, as in Belfast, they get blamed for stories that were never theirs to begin with.
